Design

       The function of the molecular governor arises from flux regulation through a permeable membrane. Therefore, the membrane is a key element in our system, and a design method of membrane with an appropriate transmission rate has to be established. In this section, we summarize how to design a membrane with a desired transmission rate. Finally, we show a simulation results to validate the design method. As you known, it is not a trivial task to make an appropriate permeable membrane for nano-scale molecules. Rather, it is a challenging task that is why we tried in the BIOMOD2014.

1.  membrane as a key element in our device

Fig1. Image of membrane

A technical highlight of our product is to realize an integral controller for regulating a concentration of a specific DNA strand as an output signal to a desired level by using a series of DNA strand displacement reactions in combination with a permeable membrane (Fig. 1). In what follows, we explain a design method of permeable membrane that a DNA strand can transmit with a desired transmission rate.

Fig. 2 illustrates a flow of a single-stranded DNA and double-stranded DNA through a membrane. The single-stranded DNA can permeate the membrane because its size is smaller than that of the pore whereas the double-stranded DNA, which is bigger than that of the pore, cannot permeate the membrane. Although a condition whether a molecule can/cannot transmit the membrane is simply decided by the size of pore, we have to establish the design theory for realizing a desired transmission rate on the membrane, which is known to be a challenging task, but we will try it below.

Fig2. Role of membrane


Fig.2 indicates that flow of a single DNA strand and multi-stranded DNA through a membrane. As shown in Fig.2, a specific single-stranded DNA can permeate membrane since the size of single-stranded DNA is enough to move the pore of membrane. However, the multi-stranded DNA can’t permeate membrane since it is much longer than single-stranded DNA. Because of the membrane, we can regulate a concentration of DNA strand.

How to design membrane

Fig.3. Size of DNA we suppose


Size of DNA strand

First, we have to inquire into a size of DNA strand as a solute that permeates the membrane. We suppose that a DNA strand that is used in a series of reactions for realizing the integral controller has 10-40 bases. Here, for the sake of simplicity a DNA strand is approximated by spherical object with a radius of 3 nm as illustrated in Fig.3

Design theory of membrane

We used general model called “pore mode” to design membrane. In this model, we assume that DNA strand is hard sphere, pore is cylinder.

Fig4. Property of membrane

Table1. Parameter


We need some formula to determine the structure of the membrane. Transmission phenomenon of osmosis membrane is described following equations

\begin{align} \ R = \frac{ {\rm C}_m-{\rm C}_p}{{\rm C}_m} = \frac{\sigma[1-exp\bigl\{-{\rm J}_v(1-\sigma)\bigl\}}{1-\sigma exp\bigl\{\frac{-{\rm J}_v(1-\sigma)} {P}\bigl\}} \tag{1} \end{align} \begin{align} \ {\rm J}_v = \ {\rm L}_p(\Delta p-\sigma\Delta\Pi) \tag{2} \end{align} \begin{align} \ {\rm J}_s = \ P({\rm C}_m-{\rm C}_p)+(1-\sigma)\overline{c} {\rm J}_v \tag{3} \end{align}
The key elements of the above transmission equation Relate to following three transmission coefficient.
\begin{align} \ {\rm L}_p = \biggl(\frac{rp^2}{8\mu}\biggl)\biggl(\frac{{\rm A}_k}{\Delta x}\biggl) \tag{4} \end{align} \begin{align} \ \sigma = \ 1-{\rm S}_F(1+\frac{16}{9}q^2) \tag{5} \end{align} \begin{align} \ P = \ D{\rm S}_D\biggl(\frac{Ak}{\Delta x}\biggl) \tag{6} \end{align}
The radius of solution is given below expression.
\begin{align} \ {\rm r}_s = \frac{KT}{6\Pi\mu D} \tag{7} \end{align}
We can design membrane for using these expressions.

However, in our design, we don’t consider the flux of solvent because we’d like to see the ……. Therefore, we lead to equation by our assumption.

Verification of Design

Assumption


We lead to following expression with our assumption by using above equations.

\begin{align} \ k\cdot\Delta x = \frac{KT}{6\Pi\mu {\rm r}_s}\cdot\biggl(1-\frac{{\rm r}_p}{{\rm r}_s}\biggl)^2\cdot\biggl(\frac{{\rm A}_k}{\Delta x}\biggl) \tag{8} \end{align}
